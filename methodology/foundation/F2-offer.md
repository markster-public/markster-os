# F2: Offer

**The question:** What outcome are you selling, and at what price?

---

## Why this step matters

Buyers do not purchase deliverables. They purchase the future state that the deliverable creates. When you sell deliverables, you compete on price. When you sell outcomes, you compete on confidence.

"We build custom CRM workflows" is a deliverable.
"You will have a working pipeline in 14 days that books qualified meetings without manual outreach" is an outcome.

The second version is harder to compare. The buyer cannot easily get three quotes for "a working pipeline in 14 days." They can easily get three quotes for "CRM setup."

This step forces you to define the outcome you deliver, build a price around it, and create a risk reversal that removes the primary objection to saying yes.

---

## The value equation

Every purchase decision is a calculation, usually unconscious:

**Value = (Dream outcome x Perceived likelihood of success) / (Time to result + Effort required)**

To increase conversion, you can:
1. Make the dream outcome more specific and vivid
2. Increase perceived likelihood of success (proof, credibility, case studies)
3. Decrease time to first result (fast wins, quick milestones)
4. Decrease perceived effort for the buyer (done-for-you, managed, handled)

Your offer definition should address all four.

---

## Outcome vs. deliverable: the reframe

For each deliverable you currently sell, ask: what is the buyer's life like after this exists?

| If you deliver this... | The outcome is... |
|------------------------|------------------|
| Email sequences | Booked meetings from cold contacts they never had to manually find |
| A website | Inbound inquiries from people who found them and already trust them |
| A sales process | Shorter sales cycles and fewer "we need to think about it" stalls |
| A content calendar | Consistent presence that shortens deal cycles for warm contacts |
| A tech stack audit | Clarity on what to cut, what to fix, and what to build next - with a prioritized roadmap |

The outcome is always downstream of the deliverable. Define the downstream.

---

## Pricing for outcome, not time

Time-based pricing makes your price feel arbitrary because it is. The buyer does not care how long it takes you. They care what they get.

Three outcome-based pricing models:

**Fixed outcome price:** One price for one defined result. "14-day growth install: $5,000." The price is anchored to the outcome, not the hours.

**Value-linked price:** Price is a percentage of the outcome created. "We charge $X, which is typically 10-15% of the pipeline value we generate in the first 90 days." Requires you to have data to support this.

**Tiered outcome packages:** Three options at different outcome levels. Entry tier = starter outcome. Mid tier = core outcome. Top tier = full outcome + ongoing. The buyer picks the outcome they want, not the number of deliverables.

**Questions to answer:**
- What is the smallest version of your outcome worth buying? (Set your floor)
- What is the full version worth in dollars to the buyer? (Set your ceiling)
- Where should you price to feel premium but accessible?
- What does the buyer compare your price against? (Hiring, status quo, cost of problem continuing)

## Risk reversal

Every buyer has a dominant objection to saying yes. In B2B professional services, it is usually one of these:

1. "I've been burned before" - they hired someone and it didn't work
2. "I can't tell if this will work for us" - the result feels uncertain
3. "I don't have time to manage this" - they fear it will add to their plate
4. "My board/partner/CFO won't approve it" - internal political risk

Risk reversals that address each:

- For "burned before": Phased engagement with a quick proof milestone. First payment unlocks Phase 1. Phase 2 only starts if Phase 1 delivers a specific, measurable result.
- For "uncertain result": Define the exact deliverable with metrics. "If we don't book 3 meetings in 30 days, we work for free until we do."
- For "adds to my plate": Managed service framing. "You approve drafts. You never touch the software."
- For "internal approval": Pre-built one-pager for them to share internally. Reduce their effort to get internal buy-in.

**Questions to answer:**
- What is your buyer's dominant objection to saying yes today?
- What risk reversal removes it?
- Can you deliver a fast first win that de-risks the full engagement?

---

## Questions to answer in writing

1. What is the one sentence description of the outcome you sell? (No deliverables, no features.)
2. What does the buyer's work life look like 30 days after buying from you?
3. What does the buyer's work life look like 90 days after buying from you?
4. What would you charge if the buyer saw the full downstream value and trusted you completely?
5. What is the fastest visible result you can deliver in the first 14 days?
6. What is the risk reversal that removes the dominant objection?

## Offer Summary Template

```
OFFER SUMMARY

Outcome Statement:
[One sentence. Start with "You will..." or "[Outcome] within [timeframe]"]

Value Equation:
- Dream outcome: [specific, vivid]
- Proof of likelihood: [case studies, data, credentials]
- Time to first result: [days or weeks]
- Buyer effort required: [what they have to do vs. what you handle]

Pricing:
- Entry package: $[amount] for [specific outcome]
- Core package: $[amount] for [specific outcome]
- Full package: $[amount] for [specific outcome]
- Price anchored against: [what they compare it to]

Risk Reversal:
- Dominant objection: [one sentence]
- Your reversal: [what you offer to remove the risk]
- Fast first win: [what they see in the first 14 days]
```
