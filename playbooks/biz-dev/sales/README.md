# Sales Playbook

A structured pipeline from first meeting to signed contract.

- [ ] F1 complete: ICP documented with decision-maker profile

- A discovery call framework (questions, structure, signals)
- A proposal template that sells outcomes, not deliverables

- A pipeline tracking template

## Step 1: Discovery

The goal of discovery is to understand whether and how you can help - and to let the prospect understand the cost of not solving this problem.

Discovery is not a pitch. If you are selling during discovery, you are doing it wrong.

**Pre-call preparation:**
- Research the company: what do they sell, who do they sell to, what does their current presence and process look like?
- Research the person: their background, what they have written or said publicly, what they have done before this role
- Know your qualifying threshold from F1: what must be true about their situation for you to want this client?

**Discovery call structure (45 minutes):**

*First 5 minutes: context*
- Why did they agree to the call? What made them respond?
- What do they already know about you? (This tells you how much context they have and how to calibrate the conversation)

*Minutes 5-25: situation questions*

These questions surface the problem, its history, and its cost:
- "Walk me through what your current [process / pipeline / system] looks like day to day."
- "What is working well about how you do it now?"
- "What is not working as well as you would like?"
- "How long has this been a challenge?"
- "What have you tried before to fix it?"
- "What happened with that?"
- "If nothing changes in the next 12 months, what does that look like?"

The last question is the most important. It surfaces the cost of inaction.

*Minutes 25-35: implication questions*

These deepen the prospect's awareness of the real cost:
- "When [problem] happens, how does that affect [their team / their clients / their revenue]?"
- "What does it cost you in time or money per month?"
- "How is this showing up in your relationships with clients / partners / team?"

*Minutes 35-40: your solution (brief)*

Only after you fully understand their situation, briefly describe how you help. Frame it in terms of the situation they just described, not a general pitch.

"Based on what you've described, here is what we typically do for [ICP type] in your situation: [outcome statement]. Does that align with what you are trying to solve?"

*Minutes 40-45: next steps*

- "Does what I described make sense for your situation?"
- If yes: "The next step is typically a proposal. I can have that to you within [X days]. Does that work?"
- If uncertain: "What would make you more confident that this is the right direction?"
- Define a specific date and action before ending the call.

**Qualifying signals during discovery:**

Green lights (they are a buyer):
- They have a specific timeline or deadline
- They have tried to solve this before (budget has been allocated in the past)
- They mention a cost or consequence of the problem continuing
- They ask about pricing without being prompted

Yellow lights (might be a buyer, needs more):
- Vague on timeline: "sometime this year"
- Has not tried to solve it before: could mean the problem is not painful enough
- Decision-maker is not on the call

Red lights (not a buyer right now):
- No urgency: cannot articulate why now
- Internal politics blocking: "we would need to get buy-in from three other people"
- Budget is not approved: "we would have to get approval"

## Step 2: Proposal

A proposal is not a scope document. It is a sales document.

Use the [proposal template](templates/proposal.md).

**Proposal structure:**

1. **Situation summary** - demonstrate that you understood their specific situation from discovery. This is not boilerplate. It references what they told you.

2. **The outcome** - what they will have or achieve as a result of working with you. Outcome, not deliverable list.

3. **The approach** - how you get there. Brief. Enough to give confidence without becoming a free consultation document.

4. **Timeline** - when they will see the first result, when the full outcome is delivered.

5. **Investment** - the price. Present it confidently, tied to the outcome value.

6. **Risk reversal** - from F2. What reduces their risk.

7. **Next steps** - what to do to say yes. One action.

**Proposal sending rules:**
- Send within 24-48 hours of the discovery call. Longer than that and momentum fades.
- Walk them through the proposal on a call rather than just sending a PDF. Proposals sent without a walkthrough close at a much lower rate.
- Ask for a decision timeline before ending the proposal review call: "When do you expect to make a decision?"

## Step 3: Close

**When they need to "think about it":**

"Thinking about it" almost always means one of three things: they are not convinced the outcome is real, they need to get internal approval, or the price feels high relative to their confidence in the result.

Ask: "What would make you more confident this is the right move?"

Then address what they say directly.

**When they need internal approval:**

Make it easy for them to sell internally. Offer to create a one-pager for them to share with their decision-maker. Write it for them. Take the work off their plate.

**When they stall after saying yes:**

Define a deadline. "I can hold this pricing and timeline until [specific date]. After that, I'll need to reassess availability."

**When they say no:**

Ask why. Not to counter-argue - to learn. "Totally understand. Can you tell me what was missing or what made it not the right fit?" The answer improves your next pitch.

## Pipeline tracking

Track every active opportunity in your CRM with:
- Company and contact name
- Deal size (estimated)
- Stage: Discovery / Proposal Sent / Proposal Reviewed / Contract Sent / Closed
- Next action and date
- Notes from last conversation

Review pipeline weekly. Every deal without a next action date will stall.
