# Discovery Call Guide

A reference sheet for running a structured 45-minute discovery call.

Print this or keep it open on a second screen. Do not read from it - use it to stay on track.

---

## Before the call (10 minutes of prep)

- [ ] Research the company: what they sell, to whom, what their current presence looks like
- [ ] Research the contact: their background, what they have said publicly, what they have built
- [ ] Identify the qualifying threshold from F1: what must be true for this to be a good fit?
- [ ] Know your discovery red lines: what would make you not want this client?

---

## Call structure

### Open (5 min)

Set the agenda. Take control early or it becomes a pitch immediately.

"Thanks for making time. I have about [X] questions to understand your situation - and at some point I'll share what we do and whether it applies. Does that work?"

Then ask: "What made you agree to this call? What were you hoping to learn?"

Their answer tells you everything about their awareness level and urgency.

---

### Situation questions (20 min)

Ask in this order. Do not rush. Let them talk.

**Current state:**
- "Walk me through what [the relevant process / pipeline / system] looks like for you right now."
- "What is working well about how you do it today?"
- "What is not working as well as you would like?"

**History:**
- "How long has [the problem they named] been a challenge?"
- "What have you tried before to solve it?"
- "What happened with that? Why did it not stick?"

**Future state:**
- "If nothing changes over the next 12 months, what does that look like for you?"

**Note:** The last question is the most important. It makes the cost of inaction tangible. Do not skip it.

---

### Implication questions (10 min)

Only after they have described the problem clearly:

- "When [specific problem they named] happens, how does that affect your team / revenue / client relationships?"
- "What does this cost you - in time or in dollars - per month?"
- "How does this show up in your [biggest priority they mentioned]?"

Your goal: they should be able to articulate the cost of inaction in their own words. You will reference this in the proposal.

---

### Your solution (5 min)

Keep this brief. Frame it entirely in terms of what they told you.

"Based on what you've described - specifically [reference 1-2 things they said] - here is what we do:

[Outcome statement from F2 - tailored to their specific situation]

We do this by [brief description of your approach - 2-3 sentences].

Most clients in your situation see [specific result from F3 proof point] within [timeframe].

Does that align with what you're trying to solve?"

---

### Next steps (5 min)

Do not end without a defined next step.

If discovery went well and they match F1:
"The next step is a proposal. I'll have that to you by [specific date]. Does it make more sense to walk through it together or would you prefer to review it first?"

If they are not ready:
"It sounds like [specific situation - timing, budget, internal approval] needs to be in place first. When does that change?"

Always get a date. Not "stay in touch" - a specific date.

---

## Signals to track during the call

Write these down. They go in the proposal.

- The exact language they used to describe their problem
- The specific cost or consequence they named
- Their timeline (if any)
- Who else is involved in the decision
- What they have tried before and why it did not work
- Their most important priority right now

---

## After the call (within 24 hours)

- [ ] Add notes to CRM (situation, cost, next steps)
- [ ] Draft proposal or schedule proposal call
- [ ] Send a brief email confirming the next step: "Good to talk - as discussed, I'll send the proposal by [date]."
