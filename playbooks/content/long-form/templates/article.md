# Article Template

Use this for LinkedIn articles, website posts, and long-form guides (600-1500 words).

Variables:
- `[ICP_SITUATION]` - the specific situation your ICP faces (from F1)
- `[COMMON_BELIEF]` - the thing most people in your category believe or do
- `[COUNTERPOINT]` - your actual position or insight
- `[EVIDENCE]` - your experience, client data, or logical argument
- `[PRACTICAL_TAKEAWAY]` - the one thing the reader should do differently
- `[CTA]` - what you want the reader to do next

---

## Structure

### Title

Options:
- "Why [COMMON_BELIEF] is keeping [ICP type] stuck at [specific problem]"
- "[Number] things I've learned from [number] [ICP type] clients about [topic]"
- "The [specific thing] most [ICP type] get wrong about [topic]"
- "What changes when you [COUNTERPOINT]"

---

### Opening paragraph (hook)

Start with the specific scenario. Name the exact situation your ICP is in. Not a trend. Not a statistic. A scene.

Example: "You've been in business for six years. You have nine clients. Eight of them came from referrals. You have not done 'real marketing' yet because the referrals kept coming. And now they are not. Not dramatically - just slower than before. And you are realizing you do not actually know how to generate a meeting with someone who has never heard of you."

The reader should think: "How did they know that?"

---

### Setup: what most people do

Describe the conventional approach or the common belief. Be specific and fair about it - do not strawman. The reader may currently be doing this thing, so describe it accurately.

---

### The problem with the conventional approach

Explain specifically why it breaks down, stalls, or produces mediocre results. Use your experience. Reference a pattern you have seen across clients or conversations.

---

### The counterpoint or new angle

Introduce your actual position. This is the core insight. State it directly.

Use evidence: a client situation (anonymized), a pattern you observed, data you have access to, or a logical argument that holds up to scrutiny.

---

### The practical breakdown

Make it concrete and usable. Give the reader a framework, a set of questions, or a sequence of actions.

Structure as numbered steps, a decision framework, or a before/after comparison. Something they can use this week, not just think about.

---

### Closing

Summarize the insight in 1-2 sentences. Not a recap of everything - just the core idea, restated with more weight now that you have built the argument.

---

### CTA

One action. Make it specific and easy.

Examples:
- "If this sounds like your situation, reply and tell me what stage you are at - I read every reply."
- "I wrote a fuller version of this in [LINKED_RESOURCE]. Worth reading if you are in the early stages of building this."
- "If you want to work through the [PRACTICAL_TAKEAWAY] for your own company, [CALENDAR_LINK] - I do a free 20-minute session on this."

---

## Notes on tone

- Write the way you talk. Use contractions. Avoid formal academic structure.
- Do not hedge everything. Take a position. "In my experience, X tends to be true" is fine. "Some people might say X could possibly be helpful in certain situations" is not.
- Short paragraphs. No paragraph longer than 4 sentences.
- If you are tempted to use the word "leverage" or "synergy," replace it with the actual thing you mean.
